AI adoption is wide; operational scale is where it breaks

What happened: 90% of retailers report using AI, but the same research says few are ready to run it at scale — execution at the store and edge level is the gap. Tractor Supply is deploying AI to help managers build last-mile delivery routes, with further improvements in the works. Grocery Outlet is rolling out Afresh's ordering technology across all store categories to manage its fast-rotating assortment. Supply Chain Dive flagged that supply chain leaders have more data than ever but less decision clarity — the bottleneck is judgment, not information. HBR separately argues the real AI leverage is developing employee judgment, not just speed.

What changed underneath: The first wave of AI adoption was broad and shallow — pilots, dashboards, proof-of-concepts that looked good in presentations. The second problem is organizational: AI decisions need to be operationalized at the worker and store level, not just the executive level. The bottleneck has shifted from technology access to change management and edge execution.

Opportunity lens: Vendors who solve the last-mile of AI deployment — manager-facing interfaces, judgment training embedded in workflows, store-level tooling — are sitting in front of a wide-open gap. In India, mid-market retail and FMCG distribution have AI pilots but almost no structured rollout infrastructure; that's the entry point.

US-China decoupling stress is becoming structural

What happened: Foreign Affairs argues Washington will come to regret its stalemate with Beijing — the relationship is not stable, just temporarily frozen. Noahpinion raises the question of whether Chinese corporations will enter zombie status: over-leveraged, politically sustained, but not competitively viable — a question it says surprisingly few people are asking. Meanwhile, the US DOT is launching the American Supply Chain Sovereignty Initiative, connecting cargo hubs with retailers and freight carriers via a visibility dashboard. Air freight spot rates spiked 41% year-over-year in May, driven by Middle East carrier disruptions, with relief only expected soon.

What changed underneath: Two parallel processes are accelerating simultaneously: the US building redundant domestic supply chain infrastructure under a sovereignty framing, and China's corporate sector potentially entering a Japan-1990s-style stagnation if political credit support and competitive weakness converge. These aren't separate stories — they're the demand and supply sides of decoupling becoming durable.

Opportunity lens: Air freight pricing volatility and supply chain visibility gaps are creating demand for software that gives importers real-time routing options, not just historical dashboards. The DOT sovereignty initiative signals government procurement appetite for domestic visibility tech and logistics infrastructure — a channel that is underserved by current vendors.

India deploying capital market reform and R&D spending as paired levers

What happened: India is changing bond tax rules to give foreign investors a tax break, explicitly designed to pull foreign capital into Indian debt markets. Separately, The Ken reports the government has announced a Rs 1 lakh crore R&D fund — but argues the real signal is a Rs 5 lakh crore ambition, with the speed of the announcement being the indicator of political seriousness, not just the headline number.

What changed underneath: India is simultaneously trying to deepen its debt capital markets (attract foreign bond buyers) and fund industrial R&D at a scale it has not attempted before. This is the classic emerging-market upgrade sequence — capital market deepening to fund technology self-sufficiency — being run at accelerated pace and in public.

Opportunity lens: The R&D fund creates procurement opportunity for applied research firms, instrument suppliers, and university-industry joint ventures that can plug into government programs. The bond market reform opens a funding channel for infrastructure and tech capex previously dependent on FDI equity. Startups that can position as R&D execution partners to state-funded programs are early in a window that will close as larger incumbents notice it.

BCI moves from experiment to sustained daily use (only one source — treat as unconfirmed)

What happened: Casey Harrell, who has ALS and is fully paralyzed, has used his brain-computer interface for almost three years and clocked thousands of hours of use — MIT Technology Review calls him the first "power user" of a BCI. He first used it to produce sentences with a research team in 2023 and has since used it independently.

What changed underneath: Most BCI coverage has focused on initial trials and proof-of-concept demonstrations in lab settings. Thousands of hours of real-world use by a single patient over years is a categorically different data point — it suggests the technology holds up outside controlled conditions and that user adaptation deepens over time rather than plateauing.

Opportunity lens: The power-user milestone suggests BCI reliability has crossed a threshold that makes assistive-tech commercial use cases investable. The remaining gap is miniaturization, cost reduction, and surgical access pathway — not signal decoding. Those are engineering and regulatory problems, which means the wedge is now for device companies and health-system partnerships, not just research labs.
