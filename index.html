Tariffs are locking in — and bending shipping timelines

What happened: A federal appeals court temporarily reinstated Trump's 10% global tariff, blocking a lower-court order that had halted duty collection for two importers and the state of Washington while litigation continues. Ocean shippers are already responding: C.H. Robinson's Global Forwarding president Mike Short reported that tariff concerns and higher costs are prompting an early peak season as importers frontload cargo. The White House separately signalled no concern about resulting consumer price increases.

What changed underneath: The 10% floor is now effectively locked in during the appeal, removing the judicial-reversal scenario importers had been pricing in. Frontloading compresses Q3 demand into Q2, distorting inventory signals and risking soft shelves later in the year — precisely when retailers are entering back-to-school season.

Opportunity lens: Logistics providers and 3PLs with flexible surge capacity are positioned to command premium rates during the compressed peak. For Indian exporters: tariff differentials versus Chinese competitors have widened; this is the window to build US buyer relationships before those relationships re-freeze around new suppliers.

Physical retail fractures widen

What happened: Sleep Number, a 40-year-old adjustable mattress brand, filed for bankruptcy and immediately inked a merger deal with Sleep Country Canada as the stalking horse bidder. Build-A-Bear formally installed Chris Hurt as CEO while reshuffling two other executive roles. Primark, preparing to split from AB Foods and operate as an independent entity, hired an Ikea finance veteran as CFO.

What changed underneath: Three established physical retailers made structural moves in one week. Sleep Number's failure confirms that even niche brands with repeat-purchase dynamics cannot sustain high fixed costs plus debt in a softening consumer environment. Primark's CFO hire signals it is building the finance infrastructure of a standalone company — an IPO or debt raise is the logical next step, and both require clean books.

Opportunity lens: Well-capitalised regional players can acquire distressed US brand equity cheaply right now — Sleep Country's stalking horse position is the template. For India: Primark's standalone trajectory makes it a more approachable direct sourcing partner, without routing negotiations through the AB Foods corporate layer.

Gen AI is removing the B2B sales rep from the buying funnel

What happened: HBR argues that organisations built around sales relationships and channel control are being disrupted as B2B buyers use generative AI to research, compare, and shortlist vendors without ever speaking to a human rep. A separate HBR piece contends that leaders are asking the wrong question about AI adoption — fixating on which skills to train rather than whether employees' mental models are compatible with AI-assisted work. Stratechery's weekly roundup covered Apple finally shipping Apple Intelligence and Anthropic's positioning, reinforcing that AI-native workflows are now baseline expectations in enterprise.

What changed underneath: The B2B sales funnel was already compressing; AI-assisted research eliminates the awareness-and-education stages that human reps historically owned. Sales teams justified by managing information asymmetry now face buyers who arrive fully briefed. Channel control — the moat of legacy enterprise software and distribution businesses — is eroding faster than incumbents have modelled.

Opportunity lens: Software that helps vendors surface correctly in AI recommendation outputs — effectively SEO for LLMs — is early and undercrowded. For Indian B2B SaaS selling into US and EU markets: the referral-network advantage of incumbents matters less when buyers research via AI; the playing field is structurally flatter than it was two years ago.

Sources: HBR — How Gen AI is Disrupting B2B Buying Decisions (https://hbr.org/2026/06/how-gen-ai-is-disrupting-b2b-buying-decisions), HBR — To Thrive Alongside AI, Focus on Mindset—Not Skillset (https://hbr.org/2026/06/to-thrive-alongside-ai-focus-on-mindset-not-skillset), Stratechery — 2026.24: Hey Siri, Tell Me a Fable (https://stratechery.com/2026/hey-siri-tell-me-a-fable/)

EV short-haul freight is clearing the unit-economics bar (only one source — treat as unconfirmed)

What happened: Kenvue Canada (maker of Tylenol) ran a short-haul electric truck pilot in the greater Toronto area with Fuel Transport and reported 44.7% savings on diesel costs.

What changed underneath: Prior EV freight pilots produced directional results; 44.7% is a margin large enough to absorb higher insurance, charging infrastructure, and vehicle depreciation — the three costs sceptics cite to dismiss pilot results. The case for short-haul EV now has a defensible number attached to it.

Opportunity lens: Fleet operators who can replicate this unit economics at scale will win long-term logistics contracts from consumer goods companies chasing scope 3 targets. India angle: short-haul EV logistics in dense urban corridors faces the same economics; the binding constraint is charging infrastructure, not vehicle cost — that is where the infrastructure build-out bet sits.

Sources: Supply Chain Dive — Kenvue Canada saves big on diesel costs with Fuel Transport EV pilot (https://www.supplychaindive.com/news/kenvue-canada-saves-big-on-diesel-costs-with-fuel-transport-ev-pilot/821993/)
