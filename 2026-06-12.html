Tariff law is whiplashing while war inflation compounds the squeeze

What happened: A federal appeals court temporarily upheld the 10% global tariff, blocking a lower-court order that had stopped collection for two importers and the state of Washington. Simultaneously, US Customs may return tariff refunds for finally liquidated entries by end of July — but that too hangs on a government appeal. Meanwhile inflation jumped 4.2%, driven by a war-induced surge in energy prices that is eroding real wages, and Kohl's responded to the value-hunting consumer by pricing "hundreds of items" under $20 for a June sale timed against Prime Day.
What changed underneath: Importers now face genuine legal uncertainty in both directions — duties they must pay today might be refunded, duties suspended might be reinstated. Tariffs have become a litigation variable, not a policy variable, which makes landed-cost planning probabilistic.
Opportunity lens: Customs-refund recovery is about to become a real niche — someone has to track liquidated entries, file claims, and front working capital against refunds. For India: war-energy inflation hits the import bill, but every Western importer rethinking China exposure under legal uncertainty is an open sourcing conversation.

(understand what the technology rewards, ignores, and trusts) and how gen AI is disrupting B2B buying decisions, arguing organizations built on sales relationships and channel control must adapt to recommendation systems. Pinterest signed a $4B AI deal with AWS — the largest in its history — to make discovery "more personal, visual and actionable."
What changed underneath: Yesterday's signal (consumers letting AI filter brand communications) now has the supply side responding: HBR telling marketers to optimize for machine trust, and Pinterest spending $4B to own AI-mediated discovery. Opportunity lens: "What does the model trust?" is becoming a measurable marketing question — structured product data, verifiable claims, machine-readable pricing. An agency or tool that audits brands for AI-agent legibility is a wedge that didn't exist a year ago.

When shared networks tighten, big shippers verticalize (one outlet — treat as a pattern to watch, not confirmed)

What happened: Dell and HPE are battling a memory-chip crunch upending server supply, partly by passing costs to customers. PepsiCo signed a multiyear deal with Gatik to expand autonomous trucking in "hard to staff" parts of its network. Supply Chain Dive also revisited how Fiji Water ran its own dedicated shipping route during COVID until market capacity recovered.
What changed underneath: Three different constraints (chips, drivers, vessel space), one pattern: when a shared input market tightens, large players either internalize the capacity or pass the cost through — and the internalization option is getting cheaper as autonomy matures.
Opportunity lens: Mid-size shippers can't run their own trucking or shipping lines — the counter-position is shared dedicated capacity (co-op style) for the tier below PepsiCo. In India, where driver shortage is chronic, autonomous-trucking partnerships will land first on fixed industrial corridors.
Sources: Supply Chain Dive — Dell, HPE server supply upended by memory crunch; PepsiCo expanding autonomous truck use; Why Fiji Water temporarily operated its own shipping network.
