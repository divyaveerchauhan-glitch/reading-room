AI is moving from chat windows into the workforce and the IP vault

What happened: MIT Technology Review reports AI-agent adoption is projected to surge as much as 300% in the next two years, with leadership teams now planning for a "hybrid human-AI workforce" where agents autonomously coordinate tasks across tools. HBR published the third installment of its annual research on how people actually use AI. Hasbro launched an AI studio, Sixth Wall, to do "behavioral licensing" — licensing how characters like Optimus Prime think, speak and interact, not just how they look. Stratechery argues Siri's new footing is "good enough for the consumer market" even if it isn't state of the art.
What changed underneath: The unit being bought is shifting from software seats to autonomous labour and from character images to character behaviour. IP owners are starting to monetise personality as an API.
Opportunity lens: Every mid-size Indian brand with a mascot or franchise has dormant "behavioral IP" and no playbook to license it; an agency that productises this — or the agent-ops tooling underneath — gets in before the category has incumbents.
Sources: MIT Technology Review — Learning to lead in a hybrid human-AI enterprise (link); HBR — New Data on How We're Really Using AI; Retail Dive — Hasbro leans into AI-driven licensing; Stratechery — The iPhone's Last Stand.

Brands are consolidating and standardising their supply chains

What happened: Gong cha standardised its US supply-chain playbook by moving to direct franchising, per its Americas supply-chain director. Tommy John's CFO says moving from multiple fulfilment partners to a single provider closed visibility gaps. Walmart and Wing added seven metros — including Philadelphia and Phoenix — to their drone-delivery expansion, targeting operations by 2027.
What changed underneath: After a decade of adding partners for resilience, mid-size brands are now paying to remove complexity: one 3PL, one playbook, one data spine. Visibility, not capacity, is the constraint being bought.
Opportunity lens: The consolidation wave strands a long tail of smaller 3PLs and creates demand for migration/integration services; in India, where D2C brands still run on 3–4 fulfilment partners, the "single data spine" pitch is largely unclaimed.
Sources: Supply Chain Dive — Gong cha supply chain overhaul; Tommy John centralized fulfillment; Walmart, Wing drone delivery expansion.

The state is stepping directly into pricing

What happened: Connecticut became the second US state after Maryland to bar retailers from using personally identifiable data to customise prices, with a similar bill awaiting New York's governor. Separately, steel imports are down 30% in 2026 as tariffs bolster US production — April raw and finished steel reached 1.87 million net tons.
What changed underneath: Two different levers, same direction: governments are no longer treating price formation as a private matter — surveillance pricing is being regulated at the state level while tariffs reshape industrial input costs.
Opportunity lens: Compliance tooling for "fair pricing" audits is about to be a real procurement line for US retailers; for India-facing supply chains, tariff-driven US steel costs make Indian fabricated exports relatively more attractive in adjacent categories.
Sources: Retail Dive — Connecticut becomes second state to regulate dynamic pricing; Supply Chain Dive — Steel imports down 30% in 2026.

India's fuel economics are being rewired (only one source — treat as unconfirmed)

What happened: Finshots explains the government's ₹10,000 crore ATF Price Stabilisation Fund — effectively smoothing jet-fuel costs that passengers ultimately pay for — and separately reports that E85 (85% ethanol blend) has become a reality "far sooner than planned."
What changed underneath: India is using fiscal plumbing to decouple transport costs from crude volatility while accelerating the ethanol transition faster than its own roadmap.
Opportunity lens: A faster-than-planned E85 rollout pulls forward demand for ethanol logistics, storage, and flex-fuel retrofits — supply chains that mostly don't exist yet in India.
Sources: Finshots — Why you're paying for flights you never took; Why E85 is here sooner than we thought.
